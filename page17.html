"The Art of Concealment"
Teenage tragedies and self-concluded trauma has compelled me to ask one continuous question with every fuck, every kiss, every game and every text: "Why do you like me more when I am concealed?". I figured that the case with a lot of men is the indefinite fear of vulnerability (that also consists attributes of femininity). Eventhough, parental indulgence (or completely lack thereof) is probably the main factor for such fixation; a pattern of avoidant personality is brought into most of these men who were once reared up with a household personality of such. More importantly, the interesting element of this process I find is the reason. Why are some people attracted to people who "reveal less" about themselves or perform this identity theatre of mystery and/or concealment/clout? It essentially is a very narcissistic projective process by nature. To put it simply: "when they know you, they can't create you". As baffling as that sounds, in the age of extreme dating specificity and swipe-culture, I believe we suffer from 'projective idealism'. We have become very standardized with our approach towards attraction that even when we pretend to be conscious about it, it's still hard to control. Heck, I know friends that won't date a "scorpio" or a "5'8"-er". Lists of standards and specifications come scrolling at every chance of conversation, as if they were attempting to invest in a new MacBook Pro. Conclusively, this process is extremely specific (and objectifying) that it has become almost impossible to find your "match", whatever that means anyway. So, what we tend to resort to now, unconsciously, is projective idealism. We create the people we meet in our heads before we even meet them. And the less we know about them, the more space we can create to fill in about them. That's the only way we can truly get the "perfect" image of a person, that we want. It's almost as if the entire span of dating occurs in our heads, not in physical reality. Hilarious. Hence, I personally think this is why we all "fall" for the "chase", or whatever other phrasing millenials (including myself) use to glamourize toxicity in relationships.
